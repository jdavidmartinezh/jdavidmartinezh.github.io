About

I graduated from ITAM in Mexico City with a B.S. in Economics (research specialization) and an M.A. in Economic Theory, with core coursework in economics and computer science.

Past experience includes serving as a Research Fellow at J-PAL LAC, Deputy Director for Strategy and Experimentation at Banorte Financial Group, and Senior Consultant for the Mexican Housing Ministry (focused on statistics). I am also a proud former co-owner of a small seafood restaurant near my college.

I am working toward a career at the intersection of economics and statistical decision theory. I am interested in applying my work to development and political economics scenarios. I currently work as a predoctoral research fellow at Harvard University for Emily Breza and Davide Viviano on statistical decision theory for meta-analysis of experimental data.

Working Papers

Coercive Capacity, Social Dissent, Repression or Redistribution? Evidence from Authoritarian Mexico (submitted)

Paper

Horacio Larreguy , David Martínez, Juan Felipe Riaño , Mariano Sánchez-Talanquer

We model how inherited coercive capacity shapes authoritarian responses to rising dissent. We employ novel data on repression to show that Mexican municipalities with stronger militia presence saw more repression and less land redistribution after mid-1960s social dissent shocks.

Full abstract

Why do authoritarian regimes exhibit subnational variation in their use of repression versus redistribution to manage local dissent? We develop a model in which an autocrat's localized response to dissent depends on incomplete information about the geography of people's discontent and the government's coercive capacity, a feature that authoritarian governments often inherit from the past. The model predicts that when dissent rises, rulers are more likely to rely on repression rather than redistribution in areas with strong inherited coercive capacity. We then test these predictions using novel data from twentieth-century Mexico under PRI rule. Our empirical strategy exploits a sharp increase in overall dissent in the mid-1960s and three sources of subnational variation: (1) a large-scale land reform that redistributed over half of all agricultural land between 1910 and 1992; (2) the localized presence of regime-aligned rural militias with revolutionary origins; and (3) newly digitized archival records documenting individual instances of repression over time. Difference-in-Differences and Instrumental Variable estimates indicate that after the sudden increase in dissent, municipalities with a stronger militia presence experienced more repression events and less land redistribution than areas with weaker or no militia presence. Our findings demonstrate how historical variation in inherited coercive capacity and informational frictions can shape the subnational logic of authoritarian control.

The Effect of Russian Misinformation and Propaganda

Beamer slides (preview)

Horacio Larreguy , David Martínez

We exploit events that generated quasi-experimental variation in trust toward COVID-19 mRNA vaccines during the early vaccination campaigns in the Baltic region. We show that demographic groups historically targeted by Russian state influence operations experienced disproportionately lower vaccination uptake following these events. Combining quantitative estimates with qualitative evidence, we link this differential response to the presence and activity of Russian state propaganda and influence efforts in Europe.
